    Section 1.4 The gradient

    There is a vast untold conspiracy to misinform all students of multivariable calculus that the gradient is the same thing as the derivative. We state here that the key difference is that the derivative at \(p\) is a dual vector of the tangent space \(T_p V\) while the gradient is a vector in the tangent space \(T_p V\text{.}\) To see how to define and connect these concepts, we need to briefly discuss charts and metrics.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Metric geometry

    It happens to be the case that often we wish to give each tangent space \(T_p V\) its own inner product \(\langle , \rangle_p\text{.}\) The case we will work in most frequently will be when \(V = \mathbb{R}^m\) is Euclidean space (sometimes called flat space). Here we have the standard basis of partial derivatives
    \begin{equation*} \partial_1, \ldots, \partial_m \end{equation*}
    which we take to be orthonormal. This means that if we write our tangent vectors as row (or column) vectors, the inner product is just the dot product. This is by far and away the most common situation and is the only situation in which many multivariable calculus texts consider.
    Nonetheless, there are several instances in which we will want to allow the pairing \(\langle , \rangle_p\) to depend on \(p\) and we will motivate this now, returning to it later when we discuss line and surface integrals. One of the main objects of study in multivariable calculus is a subspace \(X\) of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) defined by several constraints. Central to working with such a subspace is being able to define a parameterization \(\mb{G} : U \to X\) where \(U\) is an open subset of \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\) The advantage of parameterizations is that they give us concrete coordinates which we can identify with points on our subspace \(X\text{.}\) However, the geometry of \(X\) will not generally be reflected in these coordinates without adding some bells and whistles.
    As we saw in the prior section, if \(q\) is a point of \(X\text{,}\) the tangent space \(T_q X\) can be parameterized by taking the derivative of the parameterization
    \begin{equation*} \tder{p}{\mb{G}} : T_p \mathbb{R}^m \to T_q X \end{equation*}
    where \(\mb{G} (p) = q\text{.}\) Plainly, we observe that the derivative of a parameterization is a ‘tangent space parameterization’. This is very helpful because then we can write out tangent vectors of \(X\) in terms of those in \(T_p \mathbb{R}^m\) (which comes with the standard basis \(\partial_1, \ldots, \partial_m\)). However, one realizes quickly that for many nonlinear subspaces \(X\text{,}\) there is no parametrization \(\mb{G}\) (even near \(q\)) for which \(\tder{p}{\mb{G}}\) preserves lengths and angles. I.e., in general
    \begin{equation*} \tder{p}{\mb{G}} (\partial ) \cdot \tder{p}{\mb{G}} (\tilde{\partial} ) \ne \partial \cdot \tilde{\partial}. \end{equation*}
    The non-existence of such a parameterization can be seen by something called curvature, but we will leave this subject to future study. For now, we mention only that the map \(\mb{G}\) will preserve distance if and only if the above inequality is an equality (this will be justified when we consider line integrals later).

    Example 1.4.1. Circle parametrization.

    Consider the circle \(C\) of radius \(2\) centered at the origin and take the usual parameterization
    \begin{equation*} g ( t) = (2 \cos t , 2 \sin t) \end{equation*}
    where \(0 \leq t \leq 2 \pi\text{.}\) If we take the derivative, we get
    \begin{equation*} \tder{p}{g} (\partial_t) = -2\sin (p) \partial_x + 2 \cos (p) \partial_y. \end{equation*}
    Now, the norm \(\|\partial_t \|\) is \(1\) (here we identify a tangent vector with the vector for which it is the directional derivative) while the norm of \(\tder{p}{g}\) can be calculated to be \(2\text{.}\) This is because the parametrization is scaling the length of the interval \([0, 2\pi]\) by \(2\text{.}\)

    Example 1.4.2. Metrics of graphs.

    One class of examples of parameterized surfaces comes from considering graphs of functions. For example, if \(f(x,y)\) is a differentiable function on the plane \(\mathbb{R}^2\) then we can take its graph
    \begin{equation*} X = \{ (x, y, z) : z = f(x,y) \}. \end{equation*}
    Such a subspace is, in a sense, already parameterized as we can define \(\mb{G} : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3\) via
    \begin{equation*} \mb{G} (x, y) = (x, y, f(x,y)). \end{equation*}
    Taking the derivative gives
    \begin{equation*} \tder{}{\mb{G}} = \left[ \begin{matrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \amp \frac{\partial f}{ \partial y} \end{matrix} \right]. \end{equation*}
    Thus if we look at the tangent vectors
    \begin{align*} \tder{}{\mb{G}} ( \partial_x ) \amp = \partial_x + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \partial_z \\ \tder{}{\mb{G}} ( \partial_y) \amp = \partial_y + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \partial_z \end{align*}
    we see that their dot product is
    \begin{equation*} \tder{}{\mb{G}} ( \partial_x ) \cdot \tder{}{\mb{G}} ( \partial_y) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} . \end{equation*}
    This is generally non-zero, even though \(\partial_x \cdot \partial_y = 0\text{.}\) This is again because the image tangent vectors fail to be orthogonal.
    With these examples in mind, it may seem to be a hopeless task to recover the geometry of \(X\) using the coordinates given by a parameterization. However, there is a way to do this, we just need to introduce a new inner product \(\langle , \rangle_p\) for every tangent space \(T_p U\) of our domain \(\mb{G} : U \to X\text{.}\) We define this inner product as follows.

    Definition 1.4.3.

    Let \(U\) be an open subset of \(\mathbb{R}^m\) and \(\mb{G} : U \to X\) a parameterization so that for any \(q\) in \(X\) with \(\mb{G} (p) = q\) the tangent space \(T_q X\) is given by \(\tder{p}{G} (T_p \mathbb{R}^m)\text{.}\) The inner product \(\langle , \rangle_p\) on \(U\) induced by \(\mb{G}\) is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \langle \partial , \tilde{\partial} \rangle_p := \tder{p}{\mb{G}} (\partial) \cdot \tder{p}{\mb{G}} (\tilde{\partial}). \end{equation*}
    It is not hard to verify that \(\langle, \rangle_p\) is bilinear, symmetric, and positive definite, so we can indeed say that \(T_p \mathbb{R}^m\) has been promoted to an inner product space. Since the derivative \(\tder{p}{\mb{G}}\) will vary as we vary \(p\text{,}\) the pairing \(\langle , \rangle_p\) will vary too. But in any case, this pairing can be written as a matrix of functions on \(U\text{.}\) Indeed, we have the following proposition whose proof is left as an exercise.

    Proposition 1.4.4.

    Let \(U\) be an open subset of \(\mathbb{R}^m\) and \(\mb{G} : U \to X\) a parameterization. Then, if \(p\) is in \(U\) and \(\mb{u}, \mb{v}\) are column vectors representing tangent vectors in \(T_p \mathbb{R}^m\text{,}\) the inner product induced by \(\mb{G}\) at \(p\) is
    \begin{equation*} \langle \mb{u}, \mb{v} \rangle_p = \mb{u}^T \left( \tder{p}{\mb{G}}^T \tder{p}{\mb{G}} \right) \mb{v}. \end{equation*}
    Note that the matrix we obtain \(\tder{p}{\mb{G}}^T \tder{p}{\mb{G}}\) is in fact symmetric as one would expect from the symmetric property of an inner product.

    Example 1.4.5. Metric for spherical coordinates.

    The world is not flat, but spherical coordinates gives us a flat domain on which to chart the world. Indeed, fixing the variable \(\rho = 1\) we may parameterize the unit sphere \(X\text{,}\) which consists of all \((x,y,z)\) satisfying the constraint equation \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1\text{,}\) using
    \begin{equation*} \mb{G} (\theta, \varphi) = ( \sin \varphi \cos \theta , \sin \varphi \sin \theta , \cos \varphi ). \end{equation*}
    This is a function from a rectangle \(R\) in the plane to the sphere (and is a parametrization on the interior of \(R\))
    \begin{equation*} \mb{G} : [0 , 2\pi ] \times [0, \pi] \to X. \end{equation*}
    The horizontal lines in the rectangle go to latitudes and the vertical lines to longitudes. We compute the Jacobian matrix of \(\mb{G}\) to be
    \begin{equation*} \tder{p}{\mb{G}} = \left[ \begin{matrix} -\sin \varphi \sin \theta \amp \cos \varphi \cos \theta \\ \sin \varphi \cos \theta \amp \cos \varphi \sin \theta \\ 0 \amp - \sin \varphi \end{matrix} \right] \end{equation*}
    which, by Proposition 1.4.4, gives us our tangent space inner products (represented as matrices) via
    \begin{align*} \tder{p}{\mb{G}}^T \tder{p}{\mb{G}} \amp = \left[ \begin{matrix} -\sin \varphi \sin \theta \amp \sin \varphi \cos \theta \amp 0 \\ \cos \varphi \cos \theta \amp \cos \varphi \sin \theta \amp - \sin \varphi \end{matrix} \right] \left[ \begin{matrix} -\sin \varphi \sin \theta \amp \cos \varphi \cos \theta \\ \sin \varphi \cos \theta \amp \cos \varphi \sin \theta \\ 0 \amp - \sin \varphi \end{matrix} \right],\\ \amp = \left[ \begin{matrix} \sin^2 \varphi \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp \sin^2 \varphi \end{matrix} \right],\\ \amp = \left[ \begin{matrix} \sin \varphi^2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{matrix} \right]. \end{align*}
    This is a special situation to be in! Indeed, this tells us that, while the parametrization does not preserve lengths (it scales one by \(\sin \varphi\)), it still keeps the axes orthogonal. One can see this intuitively by noticing that latitude and longitude lines are still perpendicular where they meet.
    One other point which we can observe through geometric intuition about this parameterization is that the surface area of the sphere (relative to the coordinates) certainly changes. We all learn at some point, for example, that Greenland is not larger than South America, even though it may appear so on some maps. The point is that on our map coordinates, we should scale area by \(\sin^2 \varphi\) and so, near the poles where \(\varphi = 0\) and \(\pi\text{,}\) we must scale down area quite a bit.
    Having motivated the need for a varying inner product on a domain \(U\) in \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{,}\) let us give the formal definition so we may use it as notation.

    Definition 1.4.6.

    A domain \(U\) in \(\mathbb{R}^m\) is said to have a Riemannian metric (or simply metric) \(\langle , \rangle_-\) if there is a differentiable assignment of points \(p\) in \(U\) to inner products \(\langle , \rangle_p\) on their tangent space.
    We will leave details of ‘differentiable assignment’ to later courses, just noting that this means the inner products, when written as matrices, should have entries that are differentiable functions of the coordinates in \(U\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.4.2 Steepest ascent

    We now arrive at our our featured definition.

    Definition 1.4.7.

    Let \(U\) be an open subset of a vector space \(V\) with metric \(\langle , \rangle_-\text{,}\) and \(f : U \to \mathbb{R}\) a differentiable scalar function. The gradient of \(f\) at \(p\) is the tangent vector
    \begin{equation*} \nabla_p f \in T_p V \end{equation*}
    for which
    \begin{equation} \tder{p}{f} ( \partial ) = \langle \nabla_p f , \partial \rangle_p\tag{1.4.1} \end{equation}
    The gradient \(\nabla f\) of \(f\) is the vector field with tangent vector \(\nabla_p f\) at \(p\text{.}\)
    One thing to note about this definition is that, although the right hand side of equation (1.4.1) is written as a number, it is really the tangent vector \(\langle \nabla_p f , \partial \rangle_p \partial_1\) in \(T_{f(p)} \mathbb{R}^1\text{.}\) We will engage in the common practice of identifying \(\partial_1 = \partial_x = \pd{}{x} \) with \(1\) when in one dimension. Let us make this definition concrete in the case of \(V = \mathbb{R}^m\) with the dot product.

    Proposition 1.4.8.

    If \(U\) is an open subset of Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^m\) and \(f: U \to \mathbb{R}\) is a differentiable function on \(U\) then
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \partial_1 + \cdots + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_m} \partial_m . \end{equation*}
    Alternatively, using the basis \(\partial_1, \ldots, \partial_m\text{,}\) one commonly writes this as the row vector
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f = \threevec{\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}}{\vdots}{\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_m}}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Exercise.
    The gradient is among the most important vector fields that one may consider. Before seeing why this is the case, let us compute a few examples.

    Example 1.4.9. Gradient computation I.

    Consider \(f (x,y) = x^2 - xy + y\) and observe that Proposition 1.4.8 tells us that
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f = \twovec{2x - y}{1 - x}. \end{equation*}

    Example 1.4.10. Gradient computation II.

    Take \(g (x, y ,z) = e^{xyz} + \cos x - \sin z\) and compute
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f = \threevec{yz e^{xyz} - \sin x}{xz e^{xyz}}{xy e^{xyz} - \cos z}. \end{equation*}
    The observant student may be shaking their head a bit at this point and asking how this is different from the derivative \(\tder{}{f}\text{.}\) After all, these computations look identical! The answer to this question is that the derivative of a scalar function is a cotangent vector field and the gradient is a tangent vector field. So while the computations look (and are) nearly identical, the gradient is a different kind of mathematical entity than the derivative.
    To see the difference even more clearly, we consider a function on \(\mathbb{R}^2\) but with a metric that is not Euclidean. Let \(h(x,y) = x^2\) and consider \(X\) to be the graph of \(h\text{.}\) We take the function
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{f}(x,y,z) = -2x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \end{equation*}
    and restrict it to \(X\text{.}\) This gives the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y) = x^4 - 2x^2 + y^2. \end{equation*}
    Now, it is easy enough to use Proposition 1.4.8 to and find that, were we on a Euclidean space,
    \begin{equation*} \nabla^{Euclid} f = \twovec{4x^3 - 4x}{2y} \end{equation*}
    However, the domain of the function is identified with \(X\text{,}\) so we should first consider the metric given by the matrix in Proposition 1.4.4. The general form for this was given in Example 1.4.2. If we take \(\mb{h} (x,y) = (x,y, x^2)\) to parameterize the graph, we can compute the matrix for the inner product as
    \begin{equation*} G = \tder{}{\mb{h}}^T \tder{}{\mb{h}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 + h_x^2 \amp h_x h_y \\ h_x h_y \amp 1 + h_y^2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 + 4x^2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Since the new inner product is obtained by the dot product with \(G \mb{u}\text{,}\) we obtain the actual gradient by multiplying \(\nabla^{Euclid} f\) by the inverse of \(G\) to find
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f = G^{-1} \nabla^{Euclid}f = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{1 + 4x^2} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\twovec{4x^3 - 4x}{2y} = \twovec{\frac{4x^3 - 4x}{1 + 4x^2}}{2y} \end{equation*}

    Observe that away from the interior region of the interval \([-1,1]\) on the x-axis, the two vector fields start looking quite different. This difference shows the importance of using the correct metric.
    In either the Euclidean or varying metric case, the gradient exhibits a very important property. To explain this, we first need a notion of steepest ascent.

    Definition 1.4.11.

    Suppose \(U\) is a domain in \(V\) with metric and \(f: U \to \mathbb{R}\) is a differentiable scalar function at \(p \in U\text{.}\) The direction of steepest ascent of \(f\) at \(p\) is the unit tangent vector \(\partial \in T_p V\) for which \(\partial f\) is maximized. I.e. for any other unit tangent vector \(\tilde{\partial} \ne \partial\text{,}\) \(\partial f \gt \tilde{\partial} f\text{.}\) Similarly, the direction of steepest descent of \(f\) at \(p\) is the unit tangent vector \(\partial \in T_p V\) for which \(\partial f\) is minimized.
    To understand this definition, recall that tangent vectors are just directional derivatives. So the value \(\partial f\) is the same as the directional derivative \(D_\mb{v} f (p)\) and we think of \(\partial\) as the arrow \(\mb{v}\) starting at \(p\text{.}\) If you think of a topographical map, you can consider a vector starting at a point on the map to give the steepest ascent if, were you actual at that location and started moving in that direction, you would have the steepest climb ahead of you.

    Proposition 1.4.12.

    Suppose \(U\) is a domain in \(V\) with metric and \(f: U \to \mathbb{R}\) is a differentiable scalar function. If \(\nabla_p f\) is not zero, then its normalization is the direction of steepest ascent of \(f\) at \(p\text{.}\) The negative of its normalization is the direction of steepest descent.

    Proof.

    Let \(\partial\) be any unit tangent vector. By Corollary 1.3.7 we have
    \begin{equation*} \partial f (p) = \tder{p}{f} (\partial ). \end{equation*}
    By the definition of the gradient this also gives
    \begin{equation*} \partial f (p) = \langle \nabla_p f , \partial \rangle_p . \end{equation*}
    Finally, as we are in a real inner product space (and the assumption that \(\partial\) is a unit vector) we have
    \begin{equation*} \partial f (p) = \langle \nabla_p f , \partial \rangle_p = \|\nabla_p f\| \cos \theta \end{equation*}
    where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\nabla_p f\) and \(\partial\text{.}\) Now \(\cos \theta\) is maximized when \(\theta = 0\) and minimized when \(\theta = \pi\text{.}\) These values correspond to when \(\partial\) is in the direction of \(\nabla_p f\) and in the opposite direction respectively.
    A slightly weaker statement, but perhaps as important, is the following result.

    Proposition 1.4.13.

    Suppose \(U\) is a domain in \(V\) with metric and \(f: U \to \mathbb{R}\) is a differentiable scalar function. If \(\nabla_p f\) is non-zero, there is a positive \(\varepsilon\) and a differentiable path \(\gamma : (-\varepsilon , \varepsilon) \to U\) with \(\gamma (0) = p\) such that \(f \circ \gamma\) is strictly increasing.

    Proof.

    Let us identify \(\nabla_p f\) with the vector \(\mb{v}\) for which \(\nabla_p f = D_\mb{v}\text{.}\) Define \(\gamma\) to be the straight line parameterized through \(p\) as
    \begin{equation*} \gamma (t) = p + t \nabla_p f \end{equation*}
    Then the chain rule gives
    \begin{align*} \tder{t}{f \circ \gamma} (\partial_t) \amp = (\tder{\gamma(t)}{f} \circ \tder{t}{\gamma} ) (\partial_t), \\ \amp = \tder{\gamma(t)}{f} \left( \nabla_p f \right) ,\\ \amp = \langle \nabla_{\gamma (t)} f , \nabla_p f \rangle_{\gamma (t)} \partial_1. \end{align*}
    At \(t = 0\text{,}\) we have \(\gamma (t) = p\) so that the derivative is \(\|\nabla_p f \|^2\) which is positive. But since \(\gamma (t)\text{,}\) \(\langle , \rangle_-\) and \(\nabla f\) are continuous, this means that \(\tder{t}{f \circ \gamma}\) is positive for all \(t\) in a small neighborhood around \(0\text{.}\) Taking \(\varepsilon\) to be a small value for which this is true, we have that \(f \circ \gamma\) is a differentiable function with positive derivative and thus is strictly increasing.
    A more refined version of Proposition 1.4.13 is to perform what is known as gradient flow. For this, we mean to solve the ordinary differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dot{\mb{x}} (t) = \nabla_{\mb{x} (t)} f\tag{1.4.2} \end{equation}
    which is called the gradient flow equation. Finding a solution to this equation would give a path for which, at every point on the path, the direction you are travelling is increasing the function \(f\) maximally. If you imagine \(f\) as giving the height of a mountain on a topographical map, the solution would give a path of steepest ascent.

    For a connected smooth curve \(\mathcal{C}\) with endpoints \(P\) and \(Q\) in \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{,}\) there is always an interval \([0, L]\) unique path
    \begin{equation*} \gamma : [0, L] \to \mathcal{C} \end{equation*}
    for which \(\gamma (0) = P\text{,}\) \(\gamma (L) = Q\) and \(\gamma^\prime (s)\) is a unit vector for any \(s\) in \([0, L]\text{.}\) Such a path is called the arc length parameterization of \(\mathcal{C}\text{.}\) This exercise asks you to show that such a path exists.
    (a)
    Let \(\psi : [a, b] \to \mathcal{C}\) be any differentiable parameterization of \(\mathcal{C}\) with nonzero velocity vectors. Consider the scalar function
    \begin{equation*} s(t) = \int_{a}^t \| \psi^\prime (u) \| \, \diff{u}. \end{equation*}
    Show that \(s(t)\) is an increasing function by finding its derivative \(s^\prime (t)\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Suppose the range of \(s(t)\) is \([0, L]\text{.}\) Since \(s(t)\) is increasing, it passes the horizontal line test and there is an inverse function \(t : [0, L] \to [a, b]\) with independent variable \(s\text{.}\) Using the \(1\)-variable inverse function theorem and chain rule, compute the derivative
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{ds} \psi ( t(s)). \end{equation*}
    (c)
    Show that \(\psi (t (s))\) is an arc-length parameterization of \(\mathcal{C}\text{.}\)
