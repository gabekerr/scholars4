Example 1.7.10. Optimization on a hyperboloid.

Let \(X\) be the hyperboloid
\begin{equation*} x^2 + y^2 - 4 z^2 = 1. \end{equation*}
Suppose \(f(x,y,z) = x^2 - y^2 + z\) and that we want to find critical points of \(f(x,y,z)\) on \(X\text{.}\) The constraint function is then
\begin{equation*} g(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 - 4z^2 \end{equation*}
and we compute derivatives to see
\begin{align*} \tder{}{f} \amp = \left[ \begin{matrix} 2x \amp -2y \amp 1 \end{matrix} \right], \\ \tder{}{g} \amp = \left[ \begin{matrix} 2x \amp 2y \amp - 8z \end{matrix} \right]. \end{align*}
Writing Lagrange’s equation produces a new variable \(\lambda\) and the three equations.
\begin{align*} 2x \amp = 2 \lambda x, \\ -2y \amp = 2 \lambda y, \\ 1 \amp = -8\lambda z. \end{align*}
We now must be somewhat careful proceeding. A common mistake is to forget special cases and thereby miss critical points. For example, one may overlook that the first equation does \textit{not} imply that \(\lambda = 1\text{!}\) Instead, we have two possible cases:
  1. First we assume \(x = 0\) so that the first equation is satisfied. If \(y\) is also zero, then we have a problem because our constraint has that \(-4z^2 = 1\) which is not possible in the real numbers. So we may assume \(y \ne 0\) and \(\lambda = - 1\text{.}\) Putting this into the last equation gives that \(z = 1/8\) and our solution set consists of points \((0, y, 1/8)\text{.}\) Returning to the constraint equation gives \(y^2 = 33/32\) or \(y = \pm \sqrt{33/32}\text{.}\) So we attain critical points
    \begin{equation*} \left(0, \pm \sqrt{\frac{33}{32}} ,\frac{1}{8} \right) \end{equation*}
    of \(f|_X\text{.}\)
  2. Now for the case of \(x \ne 0\) we may divide the first equation by \(2x\) and conclude that \(\lambda = 1\text{.}\) The second equation then forces us to assume that \(y = 0\) and the third gives \(z = - 1/8\text{.}\) Using the constraint again gives the critical points
    \begin{equation*} \left(\pm \sqrt{\frac{33}{32}}, 0 , -\frac{1}{8} \right) \end{equation*}
    of \(f|_X\text{.}\)
Now that we have found all of the critical points of \(f|_X\text{,}\) we can ask if any of them are maxima or minima. In fact, in this case none of them are as \(f(x,y,z)\) can be arbitrarily large or small on the hyperboloid (check this!). One way to see the local behavior of \(f\) on \(X\) is to take the Hessian
\begin{equation*} \text{Hess}(f) = \left[ \begin{matrix} 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp - 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{matrix} \right] \end{equation*}
and restrict it to the tangent space of \(X\) at the corresponding critical point. Recall that Theorem 1.3.13 specifies the tangent space to \(X\) at \(p\) as the kernel of \(\tder{p}{g}\text{.}\) It is left as an exercise to then see that the Hessian restricted to each tangent space gives a non-degenerate quadratic form (even though \(\textnormal{Hess}(f)\) is degenerate on \(T_p \mathbb{R}^3\)) and is neither positive nor negative definite. Thus each of these critical points are saddle points.
