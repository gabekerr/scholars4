Proposition 3.2.2.

If \(\mathcal{B} = \{ \mb{v}_1 , \ldots, \mb{v}_n \}\) is a basis for the vector space \(V\) then
\begin{equation*} \left\{ \mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{i_k} :1 \leq i_1 \lt i_2 \lt \cdots \lt i_k \leq n\right\} \end{equation*}
is a basis for the \(k\)-the wedge product \(\bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) Furthermore, if \(k \gt n\) then \(\bigwedge^k V\) is the zero vector space.

Proof.

We prove that this set spans \(\bigwedge^k V\) and leave linear independence to a more advanced treatment. Proceed by induction on \(k\text{.}\) Certainly when \(k = 1\) we have the case of \(V\) itself. Assume it to be true for \((k - 1)\text{,}\) and consider the exterior product \(\mb{u}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{u}_k\) in \(\bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) Then by the induction hypothesis, there are scalars \(a_{i_1, \ldots, i_{k - 1}}\) with
\begin{equation*} \mb{u}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{u}_{k - 1} = \sum_{i_1 \lt \cdots \lt i_{k - 1}} a_{i_1, \ldots, i_{k - 1}}\mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \mb{v}_{i_{k - 1}} \end{equation*}
and since \(\mathcal{B}\) is a basis we also have
\begin{equation*} \mb{u}_k = \sum_{j = 1}^n b_j \mb{v}_j. \end{equation*}
Expanding their product and applying bilinearity then gives \(\mb{u}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{u}_k\) as a linear combination of the \(k\)-products
\begin{equation*} \left\{ \mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{i_k} :1 \leq i_1 \lt i_2 \lt \cdots \lt i_k \leq n\right\}. \end{equation*}
The proof of linear independence relies on the construction of the exterior algebra from tensor products which we will leave to an abstract algebra course.
