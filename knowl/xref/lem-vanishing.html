Lemma 1.3.3.

Suppose \(U_1\) and \(U_2\) are neighborhoods of \(\mb{c}_1\) and \(\mb{c}_2\) in inner product spaces \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) and \(\mb{H} : U_1 \to U_2\) is differentiable at \(\mb{c}_1\) with \(\mb{H} (\mb{c}_1) = \mb{c}_2\text{.}\) If \(E : U_2 \to K\) vanishes higher than first order at \(\mb{c}_2\) then \(E \circ \mb{H}\) vanishes higher than first order at \(\mb{c}_2\text{.}\)

Proof.

By definition, for any \(\tilde{\varepsilon} > 0\) there is a \(\delta\) so that if \(\| \mb{y} - \mb{c}_2\| \lt \delta\) then \(\|E (\mb{y} )\| \lt \tilde{\varepsilon} \| \mb{y} - \mb{c}_2\|\text{.}\) Using the continuity of \(\mb{H}\) and LemmaÂ 1.3.1 we have that there is a \(C\) and, in a neighborhood of \(\mb{c}_1\) (which maps to an appropriate neighborhood of \(\mb{c}_2\)),
\begin{equation*} 0 \leq \frac{\|E ( \mb{H} (\mb{x} ))\|}{\| \mb{x} - \mb{c}_1\|} \lt \tilde{\varepsilon} \| \mb{H} ( \mb{x} ) - \mb{H} ( \mb{c}_1) \|{\|\mb{x} - \mb{c}_1\|} \lt \tilde{\varepsilon} C. \end{equation*}
Taking \(\delta\) for \(\varepsilon / C \) gives the result.
