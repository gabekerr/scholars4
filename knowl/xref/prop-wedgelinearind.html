Proposition 3.1.4.

Let \(\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_k\) be vectors in \(V\text{.}\) Then they are linearly independent if and only if
\begin{equation*} \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k \ne 0 \end{equation*}
in \(\bigwedge^* V\text{.}\)

Proof.

If \(\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_k\) are linearly independent, then they can be extended to a basis of \(V\text{.}\) But then Proposition~\ref{prop:wedgebasis} asserts that \(\mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k\) is a vector in a basis for \(\bigwedge^k V\) and thus is non-zero. If they are linearly dependent, then we may assume that, after reordering, \(\mb{v}_k\) can be written as a linear combination
\begin{equation*} \mb{v}_k = a_1 \mb{v}_1 + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \mb{v}_{k - 1}. \end{equation*}
But then using bilinearity we have
\begin{align*} \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k \amp = \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1} \wedge (a_1 \mb{v}_1 + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \mb{v}_{k - 1} ), \\ \amp = a_1 \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1} \wedge \mb{v}_1 + \cdots + a_{k - 1}\mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1} \wedge . \mb{v}_{k - 1}. \end{align*}
But every summand in the last expression has a wedge product of two \(\mb{v}_i\)â€™s, so using anti-commutativity, we get that each of these is \(\mb{0}\) and we are done.
