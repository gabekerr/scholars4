Example 1.7.1. Optimization on a circle I.

Consider the closed unit disc
\begin{equation*} D = \left\{ (x, y) : x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \right\} \end{equation*}
which has interior the open unit disc and boundary the circle. By the Extreme Value Theorem, any continuous function has a maximum and minimum value on \(D\text{.}\) Take the linear scalar function
\begin{equation*} f(x,y) = x + y. \end{equation*}
If we compute the derivative of \(f\) we get
\begin{equation*} \tder{}{f} = \left[ \begin{matrix} 1 \amp 1 \end{matrix} \right]. \end{equation*}
So there are no solutions to \(\nabla f = \mb{0}\) and no extrema on \(\textnormal{Int} (D)\text{.}\) This means that the extreme values \textit{must} be on the boundary of \(D\) which is the unit circle \(S^1\text{.}\)
So now we must maximize \(f(x,y)\) while on the curve given by the constraint
\begin{equation*} g(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 = 1. \end{equation*}
In our case, we are lucky in that we have a parameterization of the boundary in hand
\begin{equation*} \mb{r} (t) = ( \cos t , \sin t ) , \hspace{.5in} 0 \leq t \leq 2 \pi. \end{equation*}
Composing with \(f\) we obtain
\begin{equation*} (f \circ \mb{r} ) (t) = \cos t + \sin t \end{equation*}
and taking derivatives we have
\begin{equation*} (f \circ \mb{r} )^\prime (t) = -\sin t + \cos t. \end{equation*}
So the critical points of \(f \circ \mb{r}\) are exactly when \(\cos t = \sin t\) (just setting the derivative to zero as in one variable calculus). This occurs for \(t = \frac{\pi}{4}\) or \(\frac{5\pi}{4}\) and the values of \(f \circ \mb{r}\) at these two points are \(\sqrt{2}\) and \(- \sqrt{2}\) respectively.
Thus when we restrict \(f\) to the boundary, we have maximum value \(\sqrt{2}\) and minimum value \(-\sqrt{2}\text{.}\) As the extremes of \(f\) on \(D\) occur on the boundary, these must be the extreme values of \(f\) on \(D\text{.}\)
