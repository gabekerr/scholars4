    Section 2.3 Integration over compact domains in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

    In this section, we extend the techniques established for integrating in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) to \(n\)-dimensional space. We start by extending Definition 2.2.4 to higher dimensions.

    Definition 2.3.1.

    A compact domain \(D\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is bounded by continuous graphs of \(x_i\) if there is a compact domain \(E\) in \(\mathbb{R}^{n - 1}\) and continuous functions \(g_1, g_2 : E \to \mathbb{R}\) for which
    \begin{equation*} D = \left\{ (x_1, \ldots, x_n) : \mb{x} = (x_1, \dots, x_{i - 1}, x_{i + 1}, \ldots , x_n) \in E , g_1 (\mb{x}) \leq x_i \leq g_2 (\mb{x}) \right\}. \end{equation*}
    Again we may use this to compute multiple integrals.

    Theorem 2.3.2.

    If \(D\) is a domain bounded by continuous graphs of \(x_i\) so that
    \begin{equation*} D = \left\{ (x_1, \ldots, x_n) : \mb{x} = (x_1, \dots, x_{i - 1}, x_{i + 1}, \ldots , x_n) \in E , g_1 (\mb{x}) \leq x_i \leq g_2 (\mb{x}) \right\}. \end{equation*}
    and \(f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)\) is a continuous function on \(D\) then
    \begin{equation*} \idotsint_D f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \, \tder{}{V} = \idotsint_E \left( \int_{g_1(\mb{x})}^{g_2(\mb{x})} f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \, \tder{}{x_i} \right) \, \tder{}{V}. \end{equation*}
    Notice that in this theorem, we are obtaining a recursive procedure for computing a multiple integral. We start with a multiple integral over an \(n\)-dimensional domain \(D\) and produce a multiple over an \((n - 1)\)-dimensional domain. Repeating this procedure will, in principle, allow one to compute the original multiple integral (assuming we do not hit a truly difficult integral!). Let us consider a few examples and then make some philosophical and practical comments!

    Example 2.3.3. .

    Let’s verify that the volume of a three dimensional ball \(B\) of radius \(R\) is indeed what we think it is. Here
    \begin{equation*} B = \{ (x,y, z) : x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq R^2 \} \end{equation*}
    can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} B = \left\{ (x,y, z) : (x,y) \in D, - \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2} \leq z \leq \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2} \right\} \end{equation*}
    where \(D\) is the disc of radius \(R\text{.}\) Applying our theorem then gives
    \begin{align*} \text{Vol} (B) \amp = \iiint_B \, \tder{}{V}, \\ \amp = \iint_D \, \left( \int_{- \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2} }^{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2}} \, \tder{}{z} \right) \, \tder{}{A}, \\ \amp = \iint_D 2 \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2} \,\tder{}{A}. \end{align*}
    We have now reduced a triple integral to a double integral and can continue using methods from last section and a \(u\)-substitution \(y = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2} \cos u\text{.}\) To save some space, we write
    \begin{equation*} I = \iint_D 2 \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2} \,\tder{}{A} \end{equation*}
    and see
    \begin{align*} I \amp = 2 \int_{-R}^R \int_{-\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}}^{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}} \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2} \,\tder{}{y} \, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = 2 \int_{-R}^R \int_{\pi}^{0} \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}\cos u)^2} \, \left(-\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}\sin u \right)\,\tder{}{u} \, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = 2 \int_{-R}^R \int_{0}^{\pi} (R^2 - x^2) \sqrt{1 - \cos^2 u} \, \sin u \,\tder{}{u} \, \tder{}{x},\\ \amp = 2 \int_{-R}^R \int_{0}^{\pi} (R^2 - x^2) \sin^2 u \,\tder{}{u} \, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = \int_{-R}^R \int_{0}^{\pi} (R^2 - x^2) (1 - \cos 2 u )\,\tder{}{u} \, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = \int_{-R}^R \pi (R^2 - x^2)\, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = \pi \left. \left( R^2 x - \frac{x^3}{3} \right) \right|_{-R}^R, \\ \amp = \frac{4 \pi R^3}{3}. \end{align*}
    Before computing another example, we mention one of the chief applications.

    Definition 2.3.4.

    Let \(D\) be a compact domain in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\rho : D \to \mathbb{R}\) a non-negative density function. The center of mass of \(D\) with respect to \(\rho\) is the point
    \begin{equation*} \mu = (\bar{x}_1, \ldots , \bar{x}_n) \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x}_i = \frac{\idotsint_D x_i \rho (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \, \tder{}{V}}{\idotsint_D \rho (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \, \tder{}{V}}. \end{equation*}
    If \(\rho\) is a constant function we call \(\mu\) the centroid of the domain \(D\text{.}\)
    This physical concept also has a very important statistical and probabilistic interpretation. This occurs when \(\rho\) is a probability density function. Here we do not need to assume that \(D\) is compact. The idea of such a function is that it measures the likelihood of an event occurring in a certain set. The rules for a function to qualify as a probability density function are simple and we’ll write them as a definition.

    Definition 2.3.5.

    Given a region \(D\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) a function \(\rho : D \to \mathbb{R}\) is a probability density if
    1. \(\rho\) takes only non-negative values,
    2. \(\rho\) is integrable on \(D\) and
      \begin{equation*} \idotsint_D \rho (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \, \tder{}{V} = 1. \end{equation*}
    For example, if \(D\) is a disc in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) and we are throwing darts at this disc, there may be a distribution \(\rho\) which tells us the probability that the dart will hit in a certain domain. I.e. if \(\Omega\) is some subset of \(D\text{,}\) we determine
    \begin{equation*} \text{Pr}[\text{dart} \in \Omega] = \iint_\Omega \rho (x,y) \, \tder{}{A}. \end{equation*}
    From this perspective, the notion of the center of mass becomes the expected value \(E_\rho\) of the associated probability.

    Example 2.3.6. Computing a centroid.

    Let us find the centroid of the upper half unit ball \(B\) given by inequalities
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq 1, \hspace{.3in} 0 \leq z. \end{equation*}
    First note that the coordinates \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\) are both \(0\text{.}\) This can be seen by observing that \(R\) is symmetric about the \(y,z\) and \(x,z\) planes (and the integrand in the coordinates are odd with respect to \(x\) and \(y\) respectively). To find \(\bar{z}\) we use that the volume of \(B\) is \(\frac{2\pi}{3}\text{,}\) take \(D\) to be the unit disc, and compute the integral
    \begin{align*} \iiint_B z \, \tder{}{V} \amp = \iint_D \left( \int_{0}^{\sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2}} z \, \tder{}{z} \right) \, \tder{}{A}, \\ \amp = \iint_D \frac{1 - x^2 - y^2}{2} \, \tder{}{A}, \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^1 \int_{-\sqrt{1 - x^2}}^{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} 1 - x^2 - y^2 \, \tder{}{y} \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-1}^1 \left. \left( y - y x^2 - \frac{y^3}{3} \right) \right|_{y = -\sqrt{1 - x^2}}^{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = \int_{-1}^1 \sqrt{1 - x^2} \left( 1 - x^2 - \frac{1 - x^2}{3} \right) \, \tder{}{x}, \\ \amp = \frac{2}{3} \int_{-1}^1 \left( 1 - x^2 \right)^{\frac{3}{2}} \, \tder{}{x}. \end{align*}
    Performing a substitution \(x = \cos u\) and using the double angle formula (twice) gives
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} \int_{-1}^1 \left( 1 - x^2 \right)^{\frac{3}{2}} \, \tder{}{x} \amp = \frac{2}{3} \int_{\pi}^0 \left( 1 - \cos^2 u \right)^{\frac{3}{2}} (- \sin u) \, \tder{}{u}, \\ \amp = \frac{2}{3} \int_{0}^\pi \sin^4 u \, \tder{}{u},\\ \amp = \frac{2}{3} \int_0^\pi \frac{3}{8} - \frac{\cos 2u}{2} + \frac{\cos 4u}{8} \, \tder{}{u}, \\ \amp = \frac{\pi}{4}. \end{align*}
    Thus
    \begin{equation*} \bar{z} = \frac{\iiint_B z \, \tder{}{V}}{\iiint_B \, \tder{}{V}} = \frac{\frac{\pi}{4}}{\frac{2\pi}{3}} = \frac{3}{8} \end{equation*}
    and the centroid is
    \begin{equation*} \mu = \left( 0 , 0 , \frac{3}{8} \right). \end{equation*}

    2.

    Suppose \(D\) is the domain in the positive octant (the subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) where all coordinates are non-negative) and whose points are distance at most \(R\) from the origin. Compute
    \begin{equation*} \iiint_D xyz \, \tder{}{V}. \end{equation*}
