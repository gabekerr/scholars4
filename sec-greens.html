    Section 5.4 Stokes’ Theorem in dimension \(2\) - Green’s Theorems

    The next application of Stokes’ Theorem involves \(2\)-dimensional regions in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)Before stating this, we recall that if
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} = P \partial_x + Q \partial_y \end{equation*}
    is a vector field defined on a region in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) then the \(2\)-dimensional curl of \(\mb{F}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \text{curl}^{2d} (\mb{F} ) = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \end{equation*}
    Let us give the quick proof of the theorem.

    Theorem 5.4.1. Green’s Theorem - Circulation Form.

    Let \(D\) be a compact domain with boundary in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) oriented with the standard orientation and \(\mb{F} = P \partial_x + Q \partial_y\) a differentiable vector field on \(D\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \iint_D \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, \tder{}{x}\, \tder{}{y} = \oint_{\partial D} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{r}}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Using Stokes’ Theorem 5.2.7, Proposition 5.1.12 and the definition of vector line integrals, we obtain
    \begin{align*} \iint_D \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, \tder{}{x}\, \tder{}{y} \amp = \iint_D \text{curl}^{2d} (\mb{F} ) \, \tder{}{x} \,\tder{}{y}, \\ \amp = \int_D \tder{}{} \left( \mb{F}^\vee \right), \\ \amp = \int_{\partial D} \mb{F}^\vee , \\ \amp = \oint_{\partial D} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{r}}. \end{align*}
    Let us show how helpful this theorem can be.

    Example 5.4.2. Applying Green’s Circulation Theorem.

    Suppose \(\mathcal{C}\) is a circle of radius \(3\) centered at \((1,1)\) oriented counter-clockwise and consider the vector field
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} = \left[ \begin{matrix} ( \ln (x^2 + 1) - y ) \amp ( x + e^{y^2} ) \end{matrix} \right]. \end{equation*}
    If we want to find the vector line integral
    \begin{equation*} \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{r}} \end{equation*}
    by a direct computation, we may find ourselves confronted with an extremely complicated formula. However, if we realize that \(\mathcal{C}\) is the boundary of the disc \(D\) of radius \(3\text{,}\) we may apply Green’s Theorem and see
    \begin{align*} \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{r}} \amp = \iint_D \frac{\partial }{\partial x} \left( x + e^{y^2} \right) - \frac{\partial }{\partial y}\left( \ln (x^2 + 1) - y \right) \, \tder{}{x}\, \tder{}{y} ,\\ \amp = \iint_D 2 \, \tder{}{x}\, \tder{}{y} ,\\ \amp = 2 \, \text{Area} (D),\\ \amp = 2 (\pi 3^2 ), \\ \amp = 18 \pi. \end{align*}
    One should think of Green’s Theorem as asserting that the integral of all infinitesimal rotations of a field within a domain equals the total circulation of said field. As the student will recall, there is more than one way to integrate a vector field over a curve in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) which brings us to the other form of Green’s Theorem.

    Theorem 5.4.3. Green’s Theorem - Flux Form.

    Let \(D\) be a compact domain with boundary in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) oriented with the standard orientation and \(\mb{F} = P \partial_x + Q \partial_y\) a differentiable vector field on \(D\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \, \tder{}{x}\, \tder{}{y} = \oint_{\partial D} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{n}}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    This follows from the Definition 4.3.8 of the flux vector integral, Proposition 5.1.15, and Stokes’ Theorem 5.2.7. We observe
    \begin{align*} \oint_{\partial D} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{n}} \amp = \oint_{\partial D} \star \mb{F}^\vee , \\ \amp = \int_D \tder{}{} ( \star \mb{F}^\vee), \\ \amp = \iint_D \star \left( \tder{}{} ( \star \mb{F}^\vee) \right) \, \tder{}{x} \, \tder{}{y}, \\ \amp = \iint_D \text{div} ( \mb{F} ) \, \tder{}{x} \, \tder{}{y}, \\ \amp = \iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \, \tder{}{x}\, \tder{}{y}. \end{align*}
    Let’s take a look at an example using this theorem.

    Example 5.4.4. Applying Green’s Flux Theorem.

    Let
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} = (\sin (y) + x) \partial_x + (y - x^2) \partial_y \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} D = \left\{ (x,y) : |x| + |y| \leq 1 \right\} \end{equation*}
    Suppose you are asked to calculate the flux of \(\mb{F}\) across the boundary \(\partial D\text{.}\) Noting that \(D\) is a square, this would involve four ugly flux vector line integrals. However, recognizing that \(\text{div} (\mb{F} ) = 2\) and that \(D\) is a square with side length \(\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) we can apply Green’s Theorem to obtain
    \begin{align*} \oint_{\partial D} \mb{F} \cdot \tder{}{\mb{n}} \amp = \iint_D 2 \, \tder{}{x} \, \tder{}{y}, \\ \amp = 2 \text{Area} (D), \\ \amp = 4. \end{align*}
    Now, just as some vector fields satisfy the cross-partials test, other vector fields have divergence zero.

    Definition 5.4.5.

    A vector field \(\mb{F}\) on an open set \(U\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is called solenoidal (also incompressible or divergence-free) if \(\text{div} (\mb{F}) = 0\text{.}\)
    In fact, of the three terms above, incompressible does the best job at describing such a field. This is because one can imagine \(\mb{F}\) as the velocity field of an incompressible fluid. And then Green’s Theorem is saying that the amount of fluid entering a region (per unit time) must equal the amount leaving a region (the difference being the flux over the boundary of the region).
    Just as satisfying the cross-partials test for \(\mb{F}\) can be viewed as \(\tder{}{\mb{F}^\vee} = 0\text{,}\) or saying \(\mb{F}^\vee\) is closed, we can also view being solenoidal as satisfying the equation
    \begin{equation*} \tder{}{( \star \mb{F}^\vee)} = 0 \end{equation*}
    so that \(\star \mb{F}^\vee\) is closed.
    An insightful student may wonder whether we can find potentials for solenoidal fields as we did for fields satisfying the cross-partials test (on simply connected domains). However, that student will quickly run into a basic question : if \(\mb{F}\) is defined on an \(n\)-dimensional domain then \(\star \mb{F}^\vee\) is an \((n - 1)\)-form and so a potential would have to be an \((n - 2)\)-form. So if we wanted the potential to be a scalar function we would need \(n = 2\text{!}\) Indeed, then \(\star \mb{F}^\vee\) is a \(1\)-form and a potential would again just be a function (we will discuss non-scalar potentials next section).

    Definition 5.4.6.

    Suppose \(\mb{F}\) is a solenoidal vector field on \(U \subset \mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) A stream function \(H\) for \(\mb{F}\) is a scalar function for which
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial y} \partial_x - \frac{\partial H}{\partial x} \partial_y . \end{equation*}
    Alternatively, the stream function satisfies the equation
    \begin{equation*} \tder{}{H} = \star \mb{F}^\vee . \end{equation*}
    As we will see in the next example, stream functions do a good job of describing the integral curves of the flow of a two dimensional vector field. These curves are both the images of solutions to the differential equation of the vector field and level curves of the stream function.
    As a side remark, stream functions do have a more general incarnation in higher (even) dimensions as Hamiltonians. The point is that a solenoidal vector field \(\mb{F}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is precisely one whose flow (i.e. whose solution to the ODE \(\mb{x}^\prime = \mb{F} (\mb{x})\)) preserves area. Such fields with a potential in general are called Hamiltonian fields and are intensively studied in modern mathematics and physics. One reason for this is that these types of fields describe the evolution of a system of particles in classical mechanics.

    Example 5.4.7. Computing a stream function.

    The rotational vector field
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} = \left[ \begin{matrix} -y \amp x \end{matrix} \right] \end{equation*}
    is called so because its time \(t\) flow is just counter-clockwise rotation of \(t\) radians. Indeed, we know that such a rotation is given by the transformation
    \begin{equation*} \psi_t (x, y) = \left( \cos (t) x - \sin (t) y, \sin (t) x + \cos (t) y \right). \end{equation*}
    So we can check that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt} \left. \psi_t (x,y) \right|_{t = 0} = (-y, x) = \mb{F} (\psi_0 (x,y)). \end{equation*}
    The map \(\psi_t\) is called the time \(t\) flow of \(\mb{F}\text{.}\) One can easily check that
    \begin{equation*} \text{div} (\mb{F} ) = 0 \end{equation*}
    so that \(\mb{F}\) is solenoidal. Note, however, that it does not pass the cross-partials test, so it is not conservative. One can check to see that
    \begin{equation*} H (x,y) = - \frac{1}{2} \left(x^2 + y^2 \right) \end{equation*}
    is a stream function for \(\mb{F}\text{.}\) We note that this function has the property that any flow line of \(\mb{F}\) (i.e. a solution to the ODE \(\mb{x}^\prime = \mb{F} (\mb{x})\)) is a level curve of \(H\text{.}\) In other words, the field \(\mb{F}\) is tangent to the level curves of \(H\text{.}\) In fact, this is a general feature of stream functions.
    We can use our knowledge of conservative vector fields to give a criterion on when stream functions exist.

    Proposition 5.4.8.

    If \(\mb{F}\) is a solenoidal vector field defined on a simply connected open set \(U\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) then there exists a stream function \(H\) for \(\mb{F}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\mb{F} = P \partial_x + Q \partial_y\) and let \(\tilde{\mb{F}} = -Q \partial_x + P \partial_y\text{.}\) Since \(\mb{F}\) is solenoidal, we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} = 0 \end{equation*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial (- Q)}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} . \end{equation*}
    But this implies \(\tilde{\mb{F}}\) satisfies the cross-partials test on the simply connected domain \(U\text{.}\) Proposition 5.3.11 gives that \(\tilde{\mb{F}}\) is conservative so there is a potential function \(H\) such that
    \begin{align*} -Q \partial_x + P \partial_y \amp = \tilde{\mb{F}}, \\ \amp = \nabla H,\\ \amp = \frac{\partial H}{\partial x} \partial_x + \frac{\partial H}{\partial y} \partial_y. \end{align*}
    Thus
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} = P \partial_x + Q \partial_y = \frac{\partial H}{\partial y} \partial_x - \frac{\partial H}{\partial x} \partial_y. \end{equation*}
    so that \(H\) is a stream function for \(\mb{F}\text{.}\)

    \begin{equation*} D = D_4 (0,0) - \left( D^\circ_1 (2, 0) \cup D^\circ_1 (-2 , 0) \right) \end{equation*}
    in the plane (this is the set of points in the large disc with the two smaller discs removed). The boundary of \(D\) consists of three circles, \(\mathcal{C}_1\) the boundary of the radius \(4\) disc and \(\mathcal{C}_2, \mathcal{C}_3\) the boundaries of the radius \(1\) discs centered at \((-2, 0)\) and \((2,0)\) respectively.     3.

    Suppose \(U\) is a simply connected open set in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) and \(\mb{F}\) is a smooth vector field that is both solenoidal and conservative. Let \(u (x,y)\) be a potential for \(\mb{F}\) and \(- v (x,y)\) a stream function for \(\mb{F}\text{.}\)

    Show that \(u\) and \(v\) satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations

    \begin{equation*} \Delta f = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = 0 . \end{equation*}
