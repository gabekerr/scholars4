    Section 3.1 Alternating forms

    This section had to come at some point, so I apologize now for presenting the inevitable content contained within! At the same time, exterior algebras could be considered a first introduction to a very intriguing subject and quite different from what you may have experienced in your mathematical education... so I retract my apology!!

    Subsection 3.1.1 Multilinear maps

    The following definition generalizes, in some sense, the notion of a linear function. First though, recall that if \(V\) is a vector space over \(K\) and \(k\) a natural number, then \(V^k\) is the set of tuples
    \begin{equation*} V^k = \left\{ (v_1, \ldots, v_k) : v_i \in V \right\}. \end{equation*}
    When \(k = 0\) we take \(V^0\) to be \(K\) by definition.

    Definition 3.1.1.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over \(K\text{.}\) A multilinear \(k\)-form is a function
    \begin{equation*} \eta : V^k \to K \end{equation*}
    subject to the property that, for every \(1 \leq i \leq k\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{matrix} \eta (v_1, \cdots , av_i + bw_i, \cdots ,v_k ) \\ \parallel \\ a \eta (v_1, \ldots, v_i, \cdots ,v_k) + b \eta (v_1, \ldots, w_i, \cdots, v_k) \end{matrix} \end{equation*}

    Example 3.1.2. Dual vector space.

    The dual \(V^*\) of \(V\) can be thought of as multilinear \(1\)-forms. Recall that this is the vector space of linear transformations
    \begin{equation*} V^* = \left\{ \delta : V \to K : \delta \text{ a linear transformation } \right\}. \end{equation*}
    As is the case for the dual space, the set of multilinear \(k\)-forms is also a vector space with addition and scalar multiplication defined as usual for functions
    \begin{equation*} \begin{matrix} (a\eta + b \nu) (v_1, \cdots , v_k ) \\ \parallel \\ a \eta (v_1, \ldots, v_k) + b \nu (v_1, \ldots,v_k) \end{matrix} \end{equation*}
    We will write the vector space of multilinear \(k\)-forms on \(V\) as
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{L}^k (V) \end{equation*}
    In fact, multilinear forms, when taken altogether, have even more structure than a vector space. We can take the product of a \(k\)-form \(\eta\) and a \(l\)-form \(\nu\) to get a \((k + l)\)-form by taking
    \begin{equation*} \eta \otimes \nu (v_1, \ldots, v_{k + l}) = \eta (v_1 , \ldots, v_k) \, \nu (v_{k + 1}, \ldots, v_{k + l}). \end{equation*}

    Example 3.1.3. Real inner products.

    If \(V\) is a real vector space then an inner product
    \begin{equation*} \langle , \rangle : V^2 \to \mathbb{R} \end{equation*}
    is a \(2\)-form on \(V\text{.}\) This \(2\)-form has the added properties of non-degeneracy and symmetry.

    Example 3.1.4. The determinant.

    If \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the real vector space of row vectors, then we consider
    \begin{equation*} \eta (\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_n) = \det \begin{bmatrix} - \amp \mb{v}_1 \amp - \\ \amp \vdots \amp \\ - \amp \mb{v}_n \amp - \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    By the row linearity property of determinants, this is an \(n\)-form. Of course, this also has the properties of being alternating and normalized.

    Proposition 3.1.5.

    Suppose \(V\) is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space and \(\delta_1 , \ldots, \delta_n\) a basis of the dual space \(V^*\text{.}\) Then the vector space \(\mathcal{L}^k (V)\) is \(n^k\)-dimensional with basis
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{B} = \left\{ \delta_{i_1} \otimes \cdots \otimes \delta_{i_k} : 1 \leq i_j \leq n, \text{ for } 1 \leq j \leq k \right\}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    To see that this is so, one first takes \(\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_n\) to be the dual basis of \(\delta_1 , \ldots, \delta_n\text{.}\) We show that \(\mathcal{B}\) spans \(\mathcal{L}^k (V)\text{.}\) Suppose \(\eta\) is a \(k\)-form and consider the \(k\)-form
    \begin{equation*} \nu = \sum_{(i_1, \ldots, i_k)} \eta \left( \mb{v}_{i_1}, \ldots, \mb{v}_{i_k}\right) \delta_{i_1} \otimes \cdots \otimes \delta_{i_k} \end{equation*}
    where the sum is over all \(k\)-tuples of numbers between \(1\) and \(n\text{.}\) It follows then that, for any tuple \((i_1, \ldots, i_k)\) we have
    \begin{equation} \nu \left( \mb{v}_{i_1}, \ldots, \mb{v}_{i_k}\right) = \eta \left( \mb{v}_{i_1}, \ldots, \mb{v}_{i_k}\right).\tag{3.1.1} \end{equation}
    But then, using the fact that these are multilinear forms, it follows that \(\nu = \eta\) so that \(\eta \) is in the span of \(\mathcal{B}\text{.}\) The linear independence of \(\mathcal{B}\) is left as an exercise.

    Subsection 3.1.2 Alternating multilinear maps

    As we saw in the examples above, there are many types of multilinear forms. Some satisfy additional conditions that help us understand them.

    Definition 3.1.6.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over \(K\text{.}\) An alternating multilinear \(k\)-form is a multilinear map
    \begin{equation*} \eta : V^k \to K \end{equation*}
    subject to the property that, for every \(1 \leq i \lt j \leq k\)
    \begin{equation*} \eta (v_1, \cdots , v_i, \cdots , v_j, \cdots ,v_k ) = - \eta (v_1, \cdots , v_j, \cdots , v_i, \cdots ,v_k ) \end{equation*}
    It is immediate from the definition that the determinant is an alternating form, but in fact there are many others. We construct alternating forms using wedge products which we will explore from another perspective in the next section. Let \(V\) be a vector space and suppose \(\mb{u}_1, \ldots, \mb{u}_k\) are vectors in \(V\text{.}\) Given a set of dual vectors \(\delta_1, \ldots, \delta_k\) define the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A (\mb{u}_1, \ldots, \mb{u}_k ) = \begin{bmatrix} \delta_1 (\mb{u}_1) \amp \cdots \amp \delta_1 (\mb{u}_k) \\ \vdots \amp \ddots \amp \vdots \\ \delta_k (\mb{u}_1) \amp \cdots \amp \delta_k (\mb{u}_k) \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Using this matrix, we can define the wedge product
    \begin{equation} \delta_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \delta_{i_k} (\mb{u}_1, \ldots, \mb{u}_k ) = \det A (\mb{u}_1, \ldots, \mb{u}_k ) .\tag{3.1.2} \end{equation}
    While this appears rather abstract, when working in \(K^n\text{,}\) it is in fact quite concrete as the next example shows.

    Example 3.1.7. Wedge product computation.

    Consider the standard dual basis \(\mb{e}_1^*, \ldots , \mb{e}_4^*\) of row \(\mathbb{R}^4\text{.}\) Then we can calculate
    \begin{align*} \mb{e}_1^* \wedge \mb{e}_3^* \wedge \mb{e}_4^* \left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right) \amp = \det \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = -2 \end{align*}

    Show that every multilinear \(2\)-form on \(V\) is the sum of a symmetric form and an alternating form.
    Hint.
    \(\eta\)\(2\)\(\eta^{sym} (v_1, v_2) = \eta (v_1 , v_2) + \eta (v_2, v_1)\)\(\eta^{alt} (v_1, v_2) = \eta (v_1, v_2) - \eta (v_2, v_1)\text{.}\)

    Verify the statements from the proof of Proposition 3.1.5
    (a)
    Verify equation (3.1.1)
    (b)
    Show that \(\mathcal{B}\) is a linearly independent set by considering any linear relation and testing it against the tuple \((\mb{v}_{i_1}, \ldots, \mb{v}_{i_k})\text{.}\)

    3.

    For \(\mb{e}_1^*, \mb{e}_2^*, \mb{e}_3^*\) the standard dual basis of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) calculate
    (a)
    \begin{equation*} \mb{e}_1^* \wedge \mb{e}_3^* \left(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right) \end{equation*}
    (b)
    \begin{equation*} (\mb{e}_1^* - \mb{e}_2^*) \wedge (2 \mb{e}_2^* + \mb{e}_3^*) \left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right) \end{equation*}

    4.

    Show that the wedge product \(\delta_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \delta_k\) does define an alternating \(k\)-form. In particular, show that it is multilinear and alternating.
