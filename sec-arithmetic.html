    Section 1.2 The derivative

    In this section, we will define the derivative of a vector valued function of many variables
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} : U \to \mathbb{R}^n \end{equation*}
    for an open set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\) We do not delay and present the definition in the abstract setting of inner product spaces.

    Definition 1.2.1.

    Let \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) be inner product spaces and suppose \(\mb{F} : U \to V_2\) is a function defined in a neighborhood \(\mb{F}\) of \(\mb{a}\text{.}\) The derivative \(\tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}}\) of \(\mb{F}\) at \(\mb{a}\) is a linear transformation from \(V_1\) to \(V_2\) for which
    \begin{equation} \lim_{\mb{x} \to \mb{a}} \frac{\| \mb{F} ( \mb{x} ) - \mb{F} (\mb{a} ) - \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \|}{\| \mb{x} - \mb{a} \|} = 0.\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}
    If \(\tder{\mb{F}}{\mb{a}}\) exists we say that \(\mb{F}\) is differentiable at \(\mb{a}\text{.}\)
    This definition certainly needs some unpacking, but it is indeed quite beautiful in its simplicity. Before getting into this, let us mention a few points. First, the derivative is sometimes called the `total derivative’ or ’differential’ of \(\mb{F}\text{,}\) but the term derivative is used in the literature as well (see, for example, Spivak’s "Calculus on Manifolds"). There are also alternative notations for the derivative like \(D_\mb{a} \mb{F}\) or \(D \mb{F} (\mb{a})\text{.}\) As we will use these notations for directional derivatives later on, we will stick with the above for the derivative.
    Now, the main point to draw the student’s attention to on first exposure to the derivative is what it is!
    \begin{equation*} \text{ The derivative of }\mb{F}\text{ at }\mb{a}\text{ is a linear transformation!} \end{equation*}
    In fact, it is directly tied to the notion of a linear approximation. We can see this by considering the limit and noticing that for \(\mb{x}\) close to \(\mb{a}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} E ( \mb{x}) := \mb{F} ( \mb{x} ) - \mb{F} (\mb{a} ) - \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \approx \mb{0}. \end{equation*}
    Here \(E ( \mb{x})\) is the `error’ in the approximation. Subtracting then gives
    \begin{equation*} \mb{F} ( \mb{x} ) \approx \mb{F} (\mb{a} ) + \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \end{equation*}
    where the approximation is up to first order (which means dividing by \(\|\mb{x} - \mb{a}\|\) and taking limits gives equality). Indeed, the definition gives us the following fact.

    Proposition 1.2.2.

    If \(\mb{F}\) is differentiable at \(\mb{a}\text{,}\) then it is continuous at \(\mb{a}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\varepsilon \gt 0\) is given and \(\delta_1\) gives us the limit in the definition of the derivative for \(\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\text{.}\) Since linear transformations are continuous, we also have that there is some \(\delta_2\) for which \(\|\mb{x} - \mb{a} \| \lt \delta_2\) implies \(\tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \lt \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\text{.}\) Then letting \(\delta = \min \{ \delta_1, \delta_2, 1\}\) we have that for any \(\| \mb{x} - \mb{a} \| \lt \delta\) the triangle inequality gives
    \begin{align*} \| \mb{F} ( \mb{x} ) - \mb{F} (\mb{a}) \| \amp = \| \mb{F} ( \mb{x} ) - \mb{F} (\mb{a}) - \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) + \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \|, \\ \amp \leq \| \mb{F} ( \mb{x} ) - \mb{F} (\mb{a}) - \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \| + \| \tder{\mb{a}}{\mb{F}} (\mb{x} - \mb{a}) \|,\\ \amp \lt \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \|\mb{x} - \mb{a} \| + \frac{\varepsilon}{2}, \\ \amp \lt \varepsilon. \end{align*}
    Using our definition of limit, this gives us the result.
    Now, while this definition is important abstractly and useful to prove the basic properties of the derivative, it is not immediately helpful for computations. To compute we will consider a less abstract setting for the moment, replacing \(V_1\) with a Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^m\) and \(V_2\) with the real line \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) i.e. we will consider scalar functions.
