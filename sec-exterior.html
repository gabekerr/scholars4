    Section 3.2 Exterior algebras

    Wedge products were seen last section to create alternating forms. In this section, we treat them as fundamental ways to build vectors in a new vector space called the exterior algebra.

    Subsection 3.2.1 The exterior product

    For the last time, we return to linear algebra and consider an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\) over \(K\) where \(K\) is either \(\mathbb{R}\) or \(\mathbb{C}\text{.}\) We want to take our vector space \(V\) and build several new ones \(\bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) These new vector spaces consists of linear combinations of wedge products (also known as exterior products) of vectors in \(V\text{.}\) We describe the rules of such a wedge product now.

    Definition 3.2.1.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over \(K\text{.}\) The wedge product of vectors \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\) is a formal vector
    \begin{equation*} u \wedge v \end{equation*}
    subject to the properties:
    Anti-symmetry: \(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} = - \mb{v} \wedge \mb{u}\text{,}\)
    Bilinear: \(\mb{u} \wedge (a \mb{v} + b \mb{w} ) = a \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} + b \mb{u} \wedge \mb{w}\text{.}\)
    The vector space of linear combinations of exterior products of \(k\) vectors is called the \(k\)-th exterior product of \(V\) and denoted \(\bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) By convention \(\bigwedge^0 V = K\text{.}\)
    The above definition should be taken as an informal one and leaves the technical notions of tensor products to a later course. In particular, the exterior product has to satisfy a so-called universal property which distinguishes it from other algebraic structures enjoying the two defining properties. Nevertheless, let us take a casual look at products in general and arrive at a more precise description of \(\bigwedge^* V\) using some propositions.
    Consider linear functions from \(\mathbb{R}^2\) to itself. Such functions form a vector space \(V\) (the dual of \(\mathbb{R}^2\)). We can write such functions as \(ax + by\text{.}\) Of course, taking our usual product of functions makes new (non-linear) ones like
    \begin{equation*} (2x + 3y ) (x - y ) = 2x^2 - 2x y + 3 y x - 3 y^2 = 2 x^2 + xy - 3 y^2. \end{equation*}
    Now what about exterior products? First, we note that if we take any vector \(\mb{v}\) then by anti-symmetry, \(\mb{v} \wedge \mb{v} = - \mb{v} \wedge \mb{v}\) which implies \(\mb{v} \wedge \mb{v} = 0\text{.}\) I.e. taking an ‘exterior square’ of a vector in \(V\) gives zero. This combined with bilinearity gives
    \begin{align*} (2x + 3y ) \wedge (x - y ) \amp = 2x \wedge x - 2x\wedge y + 3 y\wedge x - 3 y\wedge y \\ \amp = - 2x\wedge y - 3 x\wedge y \\ \amp = - 5 x \wedge y. \end{align*}
    This type of exercise can be enhanced to the following proposition.

    Proposition 3.2.2.

    If \(\mathcal{B} = \{ \mb{v}_1 , \ldots, \mb{v}_n \}\) is a basis for the vector space \(V\) then
    \begin{equation*} \left\{ \mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{i_k} :1 \leq i_1 \lt i_2 \lt \cdots \lt i_k \leq n\right\} \end{equation*}
    is a basis for the \(k\)-the wedge product \(\bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) Furthermore, if \(k \gt n\) then \(\bigwedge^k V\) is the zero vector space.

    Proof.

    We prove that this set spans \(\bigwedge^k V\) and leave linear independence to a more advanced treatment. Proceed by induction on \(k\text{.}\) Certainly when \(k = 1\) we have the case of \(V\) itself. Assume it to be true for \((k - 1)\text{,}\) and consider the exterior product \(\mb{u}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{u}_k\) in \(\bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) Then by the induction hypothesis, there are scalars \(a_{i_1, \ldots, i_{k - 1}}\) with
    \begin{equation*} \mb{u}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{u}_{k - 1} = \sum_{i_1 \lt \cdots \lt i_{k - 1}} a_{i_1, \ldots, i_{k - 1}}\mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \mb{v}_{i_{k - 1}} \end{equation*}
    and since \(\mathcal{B}\) is a basis we also have
    \begin{equation*} \mb{u}_k = \sum_{j = 1}^n b_j \mb{v}_j. \end{equation*}
    Expanding their product and applying bilinearity then gives \(\mb{u}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{u}_k\) as a linear combination of the \(k\)-products
    \begin{equation*} \left\{ \mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{i_k} :1 \leq i_1 \lt i_2 \lt \cdots \lt i_k \leq n\right\}. \end{equation*}
    The proof of linear independence relies on the construction of the exterior algebra from tensor products which we will leave to an abstract algebra course.
    For an \(n\)-dimensional vector space we write
    \begin{equation*} \bigwedge^* V = \oplus_{k = 0}^n \bigwedge^k V \end{equation*}
    and note that this vector space has both addition and exterior multiplication - in mathematics we call such spaces algebras.

    Corollary 3.2.3.

    If \(V\) is an \(n\)-dimensional vector space and \(0 \leq k \leq n\) then
    \begin{align*} \dim \bigwedge^k V \amp = \binom{n}{k}, \\ \dim \bigwedge^* V \amp = 2^n. \end{align*}
    Thus \(\bigwedge^* V\) is not only an algebra it is finite dimensional. Now let us examine a key result that makes these algebras so useful.

    Proposition 3.2.4.

    Let \(\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_k\) be vectors in \(V\text{.}\) Then they are linearly independent if and only if
    \begin{equation*} \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k \ne 0 \end{equation*}
    in \(\bigwedge^* V\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    If \(\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_k\) are linearly independent, then they can be extended to a basis of \(V\text{.}\) But then Proposition 3.2.2 asserts that \(\mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k\) is a vector in a basis for \(\bigwedge^k V\) and thus is non-zero.
    Conversely, if they are linearly dependent, then we may assume that, after reordering, \(\mb{v}_k\) can be written as a linear combination
    \begin{equation*} \mb{v}_k = a_1 \mb{v}_1 + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \mb{v}_{k - 1}. \end{equation*}
    But then using bilinearity we have
    \begin{align*} \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k \amp = \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1} \wedge (a_1 \mb{v}_1 + \cdots + a_{k - 1} \mb{v}_{k - 1} ), \\ \amp = a_1 \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1} \wedge \mb{v}_1 + \cdots \\ \amp \cdots + a_{k - 1}\mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1} \wedge \mb{v}_{k - 1}. \end{align*}
    But every summand in the last expression has a wedge product of two \(\mb{v}_i\)’s, so using anti-commutativity, we get that each of these is \(\mb{0}\) and we are done.

    Subsection 3.2.2 Geometry of exterior algebras

    The exterior algebra is miraculous in its ability to compute geometric quantities naturally. We will start with the following result.

    Proposition 3.2.5.

    Let \(\mb{c}_1, \ldots, \mb{c}_n \in K^n\) be column vectors and suppose they form the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \left[ \begin{matrix} | \amp | \amp \amp | \\ \mb{c}_1 \amp \mb{c}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \mb{c}_n \\ | \amp | \amp \amp | \end{matrix} \right] \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} \mb{c}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{c}_n = \det (A) \mb{e}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{e}_n \end{equation*}
    where \(\mb{e}_1, \ldots, \mb{e}_n\) is the standard basis.

    Proof.

    Exercise.
    We recall that the absolute value of the determinant expresses the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the columns, so we can see that the exterior product `knows’ about their volume. In fact, the exterior product knows about many other volumes as well if we start with an inner product. In particular, if \(V\) has an inner product, we can extend it to define an inner product on \(\bigwedge^* V\text{.}\) This can be done by taking an orthonormal basis \(\mathcal{B} = \{\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_n\}\) of \(V\) and making all of their products an orthonormal basis of \(\bigwedge^* V\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \{ \mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{i_k} : 1 \leq i_1 \lt \cdots \lt i_k \leq n, 0 \leq k \leq n \} \end{equation*}
    forms an orthonormal basis. To see that this makes sense, we define our inner product more intrinsically as follows.

    Definition 3.2.6.

    Suppose \((V, \langle , \rangle)\) is an inner product space. The inner product on two pure \(k\)-th wedge products is then given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} \langle \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_k , \mb{w}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{w}_k \rangle = \det \left( \langle \mb{v}_i , \mb{w}_j \rangle \right). \end{equation*}
    This inner product can be extended to \(\bigwedge^k V\) linearly.
    The fact that this definition makes sense and can be extended to all of \(\bigwedge^k V\) follows from row and column linearity of the determinant as well as the alternating property. From this, we obtain the following amazing result.

    Proposition 3.2.7.

    Let \(\mb{c}_1, \ldots, \mb{c}_r \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be column vectors and \(\mathcal{P}\) the \(r\)-dimensional parallelepiped spanned by them. Then
    \begin{equation*} \text{Vol} (\mathcal{P}) = \| \mb{c}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{c}_r \|. \end{equation*}
    Here \(\text{Vol}\) is the \(r\)-dimensional volume.

    Proof.

    We check this by considering the subspace \(V\) of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) spanned by \(\mb{c}_1, \ldots, \mb{c}_r\text{.}\) If these vectors are linearly dependent, then clearly the \(r\)-dimensional parallelepiped has \(r\)-dimensional volume \(0\) (since it is contained in a space of smaller dimension). But Proposition~\ref{prop:wedgelinearind} then also says that \(\mb{c}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{c}_r = \mb{0}\) implying the result in this case. So suppose that they are linearly independent and let \(\mathcal{B} = \{\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_r\}\) be an orthonormal basis for \(V\) (recall this can be found through the Gram-Schmidt process). We may write \(\mb{c}_i\) as a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^r\) using the basis \(\mathcal{B}\) via the coordinate map
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\mb{c}}_i = \coord{\mb{c}_i}{\mathcal{B}} \end{equation*}
    and in doing so, we have that
    \begin{equation} \mb{c}_i \cdot \mb{c}_j = \tilde{\mb{c}}_i \cdot \tilde{\mb{c}}_j.\tag{3.2.1} \end{equation}
    Another way of saying this is that the coordinate chart \([]_\mathcal{B} : \mathbb{R}^r \to \mathbb{R}^n\) preserves distance and thus preserves volume. Taking \(\tilde{\mathcal{P}}\) to be the parallelepiped spanned by \(\tilde{\mb{c}}_1, \ldots , \tilde{\mb{c}}_r\) in \(\mathbb{R}^r\text{,}\) we then have that
    \begin{equation*} \textnormal{Vol}(\mathcal{P}) = \textnormal{Vol}(\tilde{\mathcal{P}}) = | \det A | \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} A = \left[ \begin{matrix} | \amp | \amp \amp | \\ \tilde{\mb{c}}_1 \amp \tilde{\mb{c}}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \tilde{\mb{c}}_r \\ | \amp | \amp \amp | \end{matrix} \right]. \end{equation*}
    But applying Proposition~\ref{prop:wedgevolume1} to \(V\) and using the fact that \(\mathcal{B}\) is an orthonormal basis gives
    \begin{align*} \| \mb{c}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{c}_r \| \amp = \| (\det A) \mb{v}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_r \|, \\ \amp = | \det A | \sqrt{ \det \left( \langle \mb{v}_i , \mb{v}_j \rangle \right)}, \\ \amp = |\det A | \sqrt{ \det ( I ) }, \\ \amp = |\det A|, \\ \amp = \textnormal{Vol} (\mathcal{P}). \end{align*}
    Let’s run this result through an example to see how quickly it solves some problems.

    Example 3.2.8. Area computation.

    Consider the column vectors
    \begin{equation*} \mb{u} = \threevec{1}{-2}{3}, \mb{v} = \threevec{1}{0}{-1} \end{equation*}
    and let \(\mathcal{P}\) be the parallelogram spanned by them. Then, expanding in terms of the standard basis we have
    \begin{align*} \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \amp = \left( \mb{e}_1 - 2 \mb{e}_2 + 3 \mb{e}_3 \right) \wedge \left( \mb{e}_1 - \mb{e}_3 \right), \\ \amp = - \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 - 2 \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_1 + 2 \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3 +3 \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_1, \\ \amp = -4 \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 + 2 \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 + 2 \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3. \end{align*}
    Since \(\mb{e}_i \wedge \mb{e}_j\) is an orthonormal basis for \(\wedge^2 \mathbb{R}^n\) and remembering that \(2\)-dimensional volume is just area, we have
    \begin{equation*} \textnormal{Area} (\mathcal{P}) = \|\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \| = \sqrt{ 16 + 4 + 4} = 2 \sqrt{6}. \end{equation*}
    One should take a moment to appreciate this newfound technique. It is one thing to be able to find volumes of parallelograms in the plane with simple determinants, it is another to be able to find areas of parallelograms (and volumes of parallelepipeds) in high dimensional spaces \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with simple arithmetic!
    Now, there is a connection between the exterior algebra of a dual space \(V^*\) and multilinear forms on \(V\text{.}\) Indeed, equation (3.1.2) gives a direct isomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \bigwedge^k V^* \stackrel{\cong}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{L}^k (V). \end{equation*}
    This isomorphism gives a way to equate the two perspectives.
    Happily for some, and sadly for others, exterior products can be used to give even more geometric information about a vector space. The existence of the inner product on \(\bigwedge^* V\) along with a choice of orientation induces a new duality as well. Let us define these concepts now.

    Definition 3.2.9.

    An orientation on an \(n\)-dimensional real inner product space \(V\) is a choice of unit vector \(\vol\) for \(\bigwedge^n V\text{.}\) If \(V\) comes with such a choice, we call \(V\) an oriented vector space. We say that
    \begin{equation*} \vol_n = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{e}_n \end{equation*}
    is the standard orientation of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)
    Recall that the dimension of \(\bigwedge^n V\) is \(1\)-dimensional, so there are exactly two possible unit vectors corresponding to two orientations of \(V\text{.}\) Let’s consider orientations of vector spaces in low dimensions.

    Example 3.2.10. Orientations in one dimension.

    For the \(1\)-dimensional vector space \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) we have that \(\mathbb{R} = \bigwedge^1 \mathbb{R}\) so that an orientation is either \(\mb{e}_1\) or \(- \mb{e}_1\text{.}\) I.e. we are orienting to the right or to the left (in the standard picture of the number line).

    Example 3.2.11. Orientations in two dimension.

    Considering \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{,}\) we have two orientations \(\mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2\) or \(- \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 = \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_1\text{.}\) In the plane, we can consider \(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}\) as specifying a rotational direction `from \(\mb{u}\) to \(\mb{v}\)’. So the orientation \(\mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2\) can be thought of as counter-clockwise orientation (as the act of moving \(\mb{e}_1\) to \(\mb{e}_2\) is a counter-clockwise rotation) while \(\mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_1\) as clockwise orientation.

    Example 3.2.12. Orientations in three dimension.

    As a last low dimensional example, consider \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) We still have two orientations but can write them as wedge products of the standard basis in different ways :
    \begin{equation*} \vol_r = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3 = \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_1 = \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2, \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \vol_l = \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_1 = \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_2. \end{equation*}
    As always, these two orientations are negatives of each other so that \(\vol_l = - \vol_r\) in \(\bigwedge^3 \mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) Why call them orientations, you ask? Well, if you point your index finger \(\mb{i}\) parallel to your hand, middle finger \(\mb{m}\) perpendicular to your hand and thumb \(\mb{t}\) straight upward to match the directions of three basis vectors, you’ll find that \(\mb{i} \wedge \mb{m} \wedge \mb{t}\) equals \(\vol_r\) if you used your right hand and \(\vol_l\) if you used your left (unless you have severely dislocated your middle finger!). In other words, choosing the right hand rule is tantamount to choosing an orientation of \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\)
    We will see that orientations are vital ingredients in integration of differential forms. For now though, we use them to define a duality called the Hodge star operator.

    Definition 3.2.13.

    Let \(V\) be an \(n\)-dimensional oriented inner product space with orientation \(\vol\) and \(\eta \in \bigwedge^k V\text{.}\) The Hodge star of \(\eta\) is the unique element \(\star \eta \in \bigwedge^{n - k} V\) for which
    \begin{equation} \alpha \wedge (\star \eta ) = \langle \alpha , \eta \rangle \vol\tag{3.2.2} \end{equation}
    for all \(\alpha \in \bigwedge^k V\text{.}\)
    Let us check to see that \(\star\) is well defined and understand its properties.

    Proposition 3.2.14.

    The Hodge star operator \(\star : \bigwedge^k V \to \bigwedge^{n - k} V\) is a well defined linear isomorphism.

    Proof.

    Let \(\tau\) be in \(\bigwedge^{n - k} V\) and define the dual vector
    \begin{equation*} \ell_\tau : \bigwedge^k V \to \mathbb{R} \end{equation*}
    by \(\ell_\tau (\alpha) = \langle \alpha \wedge \tau , \vol \rangle\text{.}\) It is an exercise to see that \(\ell_-\) then defines a linear transformation
    \begin{equation} \ell_- : \bigwedge^{n - k} V \to \left( \bigwedge^k V \right)^*\tag{3.2.3} \end{equation}
    from the \((n - k)\)-th exterior product to the dual vector space of the \(k\)-th exterior product. Here \(\ell_-\) sends \(\tau\) to \(\ell_\tau\text{.}\)
    In fact, this map is an isomorphism. To see this we note that if \(\tau\) is non-zero then \(\ell_\tau\) must also be nonzero. Indeed, if \(\tau\) is non-zero, then written in terms of an orthonormal basis
    \begin{equation*} \tau = \sum a_{i_1 \cdots i_{n - k}} \mb{v}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{i_{n - k}}, \end{equation*}
    there must be at least one non-zero coefficient, say \(a_{i_1 \cdots i_{n - k}}\text{.}\) But taking \(j_1 , \ldots , j_k\) to be the numbers in \(\{1, \ldots, n\}\) not equal to any \(\{i_1, \ldots, i_{n - k} \}\text{,}\) we can compute
    \begin{equation*} \left( \mb{v}_{j_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{j_k} \right) \wedge \tau = \pm a_{i_1 \cdots i_{n - k}} \vol \end{equation*}
    and so
    \begin{equation*} \ell_\tau \left( \mb{v}_{j_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_{j_k} \right) \ne 0. \end{equation*}
    But this means that \(\ker (\ell_-) = \{ \mb{0} \}\) so that \(\ell_-\) is one-to-one. Proposition~\ref{prop:wedgebasis} gives us that the dimension of the codomain and domain of \(\ell_-\) are the same so that Corollary~\ref{cor:equaldim} implies \(\ell_-\) is a linear isomorphism. In particular, there is an inverse linear transformation
    \begin{equation*} (\ell_-)^{-1} : \left( \bigwedge^k V \right)^* \to \bigwedge^{n - k} V. \end{equation*}
    To complete the proof, one takes \(\iota_\eta\) to be the dual vector given by pairing with \(\eta\text{,}\) i.e. \(\iota_\eta (\alpha ) = \langle \alpha , \eta \rangle\text{.}\) Then one simply checks to see that
    \begin{equation} \star \eta = (\ell_-)^{-1} (\iota_\eta). \tag{3.2.4} \end{equation}
    This last proof was fairly abstract, but in practice, the Hodge star can be written concretely. Before looking at a couple of examples, let’s give a general formula which determines it up to sign.

    Proposition 3.2.15.

    Equip \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the dot product and the standard orientation. If \(I = \{i_1, \ldots , i_k \}\) and \(J = \{j_1, \ldots, j_{n - k} \}\) are two disjoint sets whose union is \(\{1, \ldots, n\}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \star \left( \mb{e}_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{e}_{i_k} \right) = \pm \mb{e}_{j_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{e}_{j_{n - k}}. \end{equation*}
    In particular, \(\star\) preserves inner products so that
    \begin{equation} \langle \star \tau_1 , \star \tau_2 \rangle = \langle \tau_1, \tau_2 \rangle .\tag{3.2.5} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    Exercise.
    The sign can be determined by counting the \(\#\) of times one must pass an \(I\) index over a \(J\) index to reorder \(\{i_1, \cdots , i_k, j_1, \ldots, j_{n - k}\}\) into \(\{1, \ldots, n\}\text{.}\) The sign is then \((-1)^\#\text{.}\)
    Let’s see what this looks like in dimensions two and three.

    Example 3.2.16. Hodge star in dimension two.

    In \(\mathbb{R}^2\) with the standard orientation \(\vol_2 = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2\) we have the basis
    \begin{equation*} \{1, \mb{e}_1, \mb{e}_2, \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2\} \end{equation*}
    of \(\bigwedge^* \mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) Then one can see that
    \begin{align*} \star 1 \amp = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2, \\ \star \mb{e}_1 \amp = \mb{e}_2, \\ \star \mb{e}_2 \amp = - \mb{e}_1, \\ \star ( \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2) \amp = 1, \end{align*}
    Notice in particular that restricting to \(\bigwedge^1 \mathbb{R}^2\text{,}\) we have \(\star : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2\) is the isomorphism of counter-clockwise rotation by \(\pi / 2\text{.}\)

    Example 3.2.17. Hodge star in dimension three.

    In \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with the standard orientation \(\vol_3 = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3\) we have the basis
    \begin{equation*} \{1, \mb{e}_1, \mb{e}_2, \mb{e}_3, \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2, \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3, \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3, \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3\} \end{equation*}
    of \(\bigwedge^* \mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) The Hodge star operator from \(\bigwedge^0 \mathbb{R}^3\) to \(\bigwedge^3 \mathbb{R}^3\) just interchanges \(1\) with \(\vol_3\) (more generally, it interchanges \(1\) with \(\vol_n\) for \(\mathbb{R}^n\)). The interesting cases here are the isomorphisms
    \begin{equation*} \bigwedge^1 \mathbb{R}^3 \stackrel{\star}{\longleftrightarrow} \bigwedge^2 \mathbb{R}^3. \end{equation*}
    One can compute
    \begin{align*} \star \mb{e}_1 \amp = \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3, \\ \star \mb{e}_2 \amp = - \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3, \\ \star \mb{e}_3 \amp = \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2, \\ \star ( \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2) \amp = \mb{e}_3, \\ \star ( \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3) \amp = \mb{e}_1, \\ \star ( \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3) \amp = -\mb{e}_2. \end{align*}
    In the \(3\)-dimensional case, we have that \(\star \star \eta = \eta\) (in contrast to the \(2\)-dimensional case where we saw that it \(\star \star \eta = - \eta\) for vectors in \(\bigwedge^1 \mathbb{R}^2\)).

    Subsection 3.2.3 The cross product

    Now, in many ways, 3 is special! One of these ways is that
    \begin{equation*} 3 - (1 + 1) = 1 \end{equation*}
    Note that \(3\) is the only number with this property. In other words,
    \begin{equation*} n - (1 + 1) = 1 \end{equation*}
    implies \(n = 3\text{.}\) Do we all agree ... ? Yes ? Good!
    Because of this, we can do something in \(\bigwedge^* V\) when \(V\) is \(3\)-dimensional that we cannot do for an oriented inner product space of a different dimension. We can define the cross product on vectors \(\mb{u}, \mb{v} \in V\) as:
    \begin{equation*} \mb{u} \times \mb{v} := \star \left( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right). \end{equation*}
    Indeed, suppose \(V\) is \(n\)-dimensional. Because \(\mb{u}, \mb{v}\) belong to \(V = \bigwedge^1 V\text{,}\) their wedge product \(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}\) is in \(\bigwedge^{1 + 1} V\text{.}\) This means their Hodge star is in \(\bigwedge^{n - (1 + 1)} V\text{.}\) If we want this to be a vector in \(V = \bigwedge^1 V\) then we must have that \(n - (1 + 1) = 1\text{...}\) so \(n\) must be \(3\text{.}\) I am explaining this because in every one of the millions of multivariable calculus courses I have taught, a bright student has asked me whether there is a cross product in other dimensions, to which I answer: ‘there is something, but not a cross product’.
    Now, while I firmly believe that in \(100\) years, every college student will learn wedge products, the Hodge star operator, and higher dimensional volumes rendering cross products a strange relic of the past, we do not yet live in this idyllic future. So it is important you know how to compute cross products in order to converse with your less enlightened future colleagues. Let’s derive the formula:
    \begin{align*} \threevec{a_1}{a_2}{a_3} \times \threevec{b_1}{b_2}{b_3} \amp = \star \left( \left(a_1 \mb{e}_1 + a_2 \mb{e}_2 + a_3 \mb{e}_3 \right) \wedge \left(b_1 \mb{e}_1 + b_2 \mb{e}_2 + b_3 \mb{e}_3 \right) \right), \\ \amp = \star \left( a_1 b_2 \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 + a_1 b_3 \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 + a_2 b_1 \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_1 + \cdots \right. \\ \amp \left. \cdots + a_2 b_3 \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3 + a_3 b_1 \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_1 + a_3 b_2 \mb{e}_3 \wedge \mb{e}_2 \right) , \\ \amp = \star \left( (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1) \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 + (a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1 ) \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 + \cdots \right. , \\ \amp \left. \cdots + (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 ) \mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3 \right), \\ \amp = (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1) \star \left(\mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_2 \right) + (a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1 ) \star \left( \mb{e}_1 \wedge \mb{e}_3 \right)+ \cdots , \\ \amp \cdots + (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 ) \star \left(\mb{e}_2 \wedge \mb{e}_3 \right), \\ \amp = (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1) \mb{e}_3 + (a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1 ) \left( -\mb{e}_2 \right) + (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 ) \mb{e}_1, \\ \amp = \threevec{a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2}{a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3}{a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1}. \end{align*}
    While every multivariable calculus student sees this formula at some point, the most common way they learn to use it is to take the determinant of the following strange looking matrix
    \begin{equation*} \left[ \begin{matrix} a_1 \amp a_2 \amp a_3 \end{matrix} \right] \times \left[ \begin{matrix} b_1 \amp b_2 \amp b_3 \end{matrix} \right] = \det \left[ \begin{matrix} \mb{e}_1 \amp \mb{e}_2 \amp \mb{e}_3 \\ a_1 \amp a_2 \amp a_3 \\ b_1 \amp b_2 \amp b_3 \end{matrix} \right]. \end{equation*}
    Although the first row entries are vectors, the formula for determinant still makes some sense and can be seen to match up with the derived formula. The properties of the cross product are also quite universally taught, but again are much easier to verify in our general setting.

    Proposition 3.2.18.

    Suppose \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\) are \(3\)-dimensional vectors. Then
    1. \(\mb{u} \times \mb{v} = - \mb{v} \times \mb{u}\text{,}\)
    2. \(\mb{u} \times \mb{v}\) is orthogonal to both \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\text{.}\)
    3. \(\| \mb{u} \times \mb{v}\|\) is the area of the parallelogram spanned by \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\text{.}\)
    4. if \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\) span a plane, the vectors \(\mb{u}, \mb{v}, \mb{u} \times \mb{v}\) are oriented with respect to the right hand rule.

    Proof.

    Property (1) follows from linearity of \(\star\) since
    \begin{equation*} \mb{u} \times \mb{v} = \star \left( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right) = \star \left( - \mb{v} \wedge \mb{u} \right) = - \star \left( \mb{v} \wedge \mb{u} \right) = - \mb{v} \times \mb{u}. \end{equation*}
    For (2), we recall that in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{,}\) \(\star^2\) is the identity. Then, using the defining property of the Hodge star operator,
    \begin{align*} \mb{u} \cdot \left( \mb{u} \times \mb{v} \right) \vol_3 \amp = \mb{u} \wedge \left( \star \left( \mb{u} \times \mb{v} \right) \right) , \\ \amp = \mb{u} \wedge \left( \star \star \left( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right) \right) , \\ \amp = \mb{u} \wedge \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} , \\ \amp = \mb{0}. \end{align*}
    This implies
    \begin{equation*} \mb{u} \cdot \left( \mb{u} \times \mb{v} \right) = 0 \end{equation*}
    and a similar argument (or using (1)) gives
    \begin{equation*} \mb{v} \cdot \left( \mb{u} \times \mb{v} \right) = 0. \end{equation*}
    For (3), take \(\mathcal{P}\) to be the parallelogram spanned by \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\text{.}\) Then we just apply Proposition 3.2.7 along with equation (3.2.5) of Proposition 3.2.15 to get
    \begin{align*} \textnormal{Area} (\mathcal{P}) \amp = \| \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \|, \\ \amp = \sqrt{ \left(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right) \cdot \left(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right)} ,\\ \amp = \sqrt{ \star \left(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right) \cdot \star \left(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \right)} , \\ \amp = \| \mb{u} \times \mb{v} \|. \end{align*}
    Finally, for (4) we must explain what is meant if it is not clear. A basis \(\mathcal{B} = \{\mb{v}_1, \ldots, \mb{v}_n \}\) in an oriented vector space is positively oriented if there is a positive real number \(\lambda \) for which
    \begin{equation*} \mb{v}_1\wedge \cdots \wedge \mb{v}_n = \lambda \vol . \end{equation*}
    This is what is meant by saying \(\mb{u}, \mb{v}, \mb{u} \times \mb{v}\) are oriented with respect to the right hand rule - that they are positively oriented with respect to the standard orientation \(\vol_3\text{.}\) Seeing this is simple though. Using our defining property of Hodge star we have,
    \begin{align*} \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \wedge ( \mb{u} \times \mb{v} ) \amp = ( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}) \wedge (\star ( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}) ), \\ \amp = ( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}) \cdot ( \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}) \vol_3,\\ \amp = \| \mb{u} \wedge \mb{v} \|^2 \vol_3. \end{align*}
    Because \(\mb{u}\) and \(\mb{v}\) span a plane, they are linearly independent so that Proposition 3.2.4 implies \(\mb{u} \wedge \mb{v}\) is non-zero giving our result.
    In the coming sections, we will see that exterior products play a central role when we consider integration of forms and several fundamental theorems such as Stokes’, Green’s, and the divergence theorem.
